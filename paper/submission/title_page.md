## Condensed abstract (≤ 250 words, unstructured — for Editorial Manager)

Childhood sexual abuse is associated with maladaptive outcomes extending
well beyond common stereotypes. Using the 2023 National Youth Risk Behavior
Survey (N = 20,103), this study tested a coping-theoretic account of
adolescent substance use and social withdrawal against four pre-stated
hypotheses, combining survey-weighted epidemiological estimation with
interpretable machine learning. Students reporting sexual-violence
victimization (weighted prevalence 14.6%) showed elevated prevalence of all
twelve substance outcomes, with prevalence ratios rising monotonically with
substance severity — from 2.2 for alcohol to 7.9 for injection drug use —
alongside lower school connectedness and parental monitoring. Associations
persisted after adjustment (odds ratios 2.19–3.19; E-values 2.3–3.0), and
household adversity showed strict dose–response gradients. Causal mediation
analysis found persistent sadness/hopelessness mediated 15–23% of
exposure–substance-use associations and 46% of the low-connectedness
association. Cross-validated models using only exposure, adversity, and
demographic features achieved ROC-AUC 0.70–0.79, with victimization
variables outranking demographics in SHAP attributions; class weighting
decisively outperformed SMOTE for sensitivity. A calibrated,
fairness-audited screening prototype restricted to thirteen
intake-collectable items identified 81% of currently-using students at a
decision-curve-supported operating point. Findings are cross-sectional,
self-reported associations and likely lower bounds given under-disclosure.
The severity-graded, distress-mediated pattern is consistent with
trauma-driven coping rather than intrinsic risk-seeking, and suggests
trauma-informed early identification is feasible with information schools
already collect — provided screening routes to support, never discipline.
